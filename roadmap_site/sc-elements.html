Superconducting elements

Three flavours of SC element compared side-by-side: the workhorse transmon, the high-anharmonicity fluxonium, and the noise-clean bare LC resonator.

1 · Why a superconducting element?

A molecular spin alone has small magnetic-dipole coupling to free space. To get strong, controllable interactions we route the spin's microwave magnetic field through a circuit:

  • A bare LC resonator concentrates microwave magnetic field at the spin's location, boosting $g$ by an order of magnitude.
  • A nonlinear element (transmon, fluxonium) provides a qubit-like anharmonic spectrum: a dedicated $|0\rangle, |1\rangle$ subspace plus selection rules useful for readout and driving.

2 · Bare LC resonator

The simplest building block. A high-Q superconducting LC circuit (CPW, lumped, or 3D cavity) gives a linear harmonic oscillator:

$$ H_\text{LC} = \omega_c\,a^\dagger a, \qquad \omega_c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}. $$
  • $Q$-factors of $10^5\text{–}10^7$ are routine; 3D cavities reach $Q \sim 10^9$.
  • Photon-loss rate $\kappa = \omega_c/Q$ enters as a Lindblad operator $L = \sqrt{\kappa}\, a$.
  • No anharmonicity — cannot be used directly as a qubit, but ideal as a quantum bus.

For magnetic coupling to a spin, the relevant field at the spin's location is

$$ \mathbf{B}_\text{vac}(\mathbf{r}) = b_0(\mathbf{r})(a + a^\dagger), \qquad b_0 \sim \sqrt{\mu_0 \hbar \omega_c / V_\text{mode}}, $$

so the single-photon Rabi coupling $g = g_e \mu_B b_0 / \hbar$ scales as $V_\text{mode}^{-1/2}$. Constriction-type resonators with sub-µm field maxima push $g$ from ~kHz (free-space) into the 0.1–1 MHz range for a single molecule.

3 · Transmon — Duffing oscillator

The transmon is a Cooper-pair box biased deep in the regime $E_J/E_C \gtrsim 50$ where the dispersion of energy levels with the offset charge $n_g$ is exponentially suppressed.

$$ H_\text{T}^\text{CPB} = 4 E_C (\hat n - n_g)^2 - E_J \cos\hat\phi . $$

Expand $\cos\hat\phi$ to fourth order and quantise $\hat\phi$, $\hat n$ in oscillator variables to get the Duffing approximation:

$$ H_\text{T}^\text{Duff} \approx \omega_q\, a^\dagger a - \frac{\alpha}{2}\, a^\dagger a^\dagger a a, \qquad \omega_q = \sqrt{8 E_C E_J} - E_C, \qquad \alpha \approx E_C . $$

Single-photon Kerr $\alpha$ sets the anharmonicity: transmons run with $\alpha/2\pi \approx 200\,\text{MHz}$.

For LindbladTTN purposes, a transmon site is a truncated Fock space (typically $N_\text{cut}=4$) carrying the Duffing Hamiltonian. In v0.3.0 this is a single $d_s = 4$ End in the tree, built with templates.transmon(omega_q, alpha, N_cut=4).

4 · Fluxonium — cos(φ) on a superinductor

Fluxonium adds a superinductance $L$ shunting the Josephson junction. This breaks the periodicity of $\cos\hat\phi$ and gives access to genuinely nonlinear eigenstates.

$$ H_\text{F} = 4 E_C \hat n^2 - E_J \cos(\hat\phi - \varphi_\text{ext}) + \tfrac{1}{2}\, E_L\, \hat\phi^2 . $$

At half-flux $\varphi_\text{ext} = \pi$ the two lowest eigenstates are localised in different wells of the tilted cosine, with $\omega_q / 2\pi \sim 0.5$–1 GHz and anharmonicity comparable to or larger than $\omega_q$. This makes fluxonium an ideal substrate for fast, leakage-suppressed gates.

Diagonalising $H_\text{F}$ requires a charge-basis or oscillator-basis truncation typically of order $\dim = 40$–$100$. After M2 + M4 this becomes a single bosonic site with a custom Hamiltonian closed-form. The dimension is large compared to a transmon but the resulting qubit subspace is much more isolated.

Roadmap recommendation

Start with bare LC + vanadyl spin for the cleanest physics demonstration of cavity-mediated coupling. Move to transmon + LC + spin once dispersive control is needed (single-shot readout, parametric gates). Reserve fluxonium for the regime where transmon Kerr / leakage start to limit gate fidelity.

6 · In code: templates (M4, shipped)

Each template returns a (sites, H_terms, L_terms) bundle that can be combined with merge(). The example below builds a transmon ↔ LC bus ↔ vanadyl spin chain:

from lindblad_ttn import LindbladTTN
from lindblad_ttn.templates import (
    transmon, bare_lc, vanadyl_spin, merge,
)

# Three independent bundles
t_sites, H_t, L_t = transmon(omega_q=2*3.1416*5e9, alpha=2*3.1416*(-300e6),
                             N_cut=4, T1=100e-6, name="t0")
c_sites, H_c, L_c = bare_lc(omega_c=2*3.1416*4.8e9, N_cut=8,
                            kappa=2*3.1416*1e5, name="c0")
s_sites, H_s, L_s = vanadyl_spin(omega_q=2*3.1416*5.05e9, T1=1e-3, T2=7e-4,
                                 name_e="vo")

# Concatenate site lists and term dicts
sites, H_terms, L_terms = merge((t_sites, H_t, L_t),
                                (c_sites, H_c, L_c),
                                (s_sites, H_s, L_s))

# (Inter-component couplings g·sp·a + g·sm·a† added by the user.)
solver = LindbladTTN(
    sites=sites, H_terms=H_terms, L_terms=L_terms,
    bond_dim=16, topology="train", strategy="ps1",
)

For the dispersive limit there is a single-call helper:

from lindblad_ttn.templates import dispersive_readout

sites, H_terms, L_terms = dispersive_readout(
    omega_q=2*3.1416*5e9, omega_c=2*3.1416*4.8e9, chi=2*3.1416*1e6,
    N_cut=6, kappa=2*3.1416*1e5,
)
