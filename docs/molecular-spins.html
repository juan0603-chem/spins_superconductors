Molecular spin qubits

The chemistry side of the hybrid platform. Long T2, chemically tunable energy levels, and an enormous design space — at the cost of small intrinsic couplings.

1 · What is a molecular spin qubit?

A molecular spin qubit is an unpaired-electron magnetic moment embedded in a deliberately designed coordination complex. The molecule's geometry, ligands, and isotopic composition all become qubit-engineering parameters:

  • The g-tensor sets the Zeeman frequency in a static $\mathbf{B}_0$ field.
  • The crystal field sets zero-field splittings, and for high spins, qudit-like internal structure.
  • The nuclear-spin environment (own nuclei + ligand nuclei) drives the dominant pure-dephasing channel.
  • The chemistry lets us position the qubit on a surface, in a crystal, or on top of a nanowire.
For newcomers

Think of a molecular spin qubit as the molecular cousin of a phosphorus donor in silicon or a nitrogen-vacancy centre in diamond — but with the chemist's full periodic table on tap.

2 · The general spin Hamiltonian

For a single molecule with electronic spin $S$ and one or more nuclear spins $I_k$, the static Hamiltonian is:

$$ H_\text{spin} = \underbrace{\mu_B \mathbf{B}\cdot\mathbf{g}\cdot\mathbf{S}}_{\text{electronic Zeeman}} + \underbrace{D\, S_z^2 + E\,(S_x^2 - S_y^2)}_{\text{2nd-order ZFS}} + \underbrace{\sum_{k,q} B_k^q\, O_k^q(\mathbf{S})}_{\text{higher-order crystal field}} + \underbrace{\sum_k \mathbf{S}\cdot \mathbf{A}_k \cdot \mathbf{I}_k}_{\text{hyperfine}} + \underbrace{\sum_k \mu_N\,g_N\, \mathbf{B}\cdot\mathbf{I}_k}_{\text{nuclear Zeeman}}. $$

The Stevens operators $O_k^q(\mathbf{S})$ are polynomials in $S_x$, $S_y$, $S_z$ (and their $S_\pm$ combinations) acting on the $(2S+1)$-dimensional spin manifold. They generalise the second-order $D$, $E$ tensor to arbitrary rank — needed for high-spin lanthanides where 4th- and 6th-order terms dominate.

The $B_k^q$ parameters are routinely fitted to EPR, AC susceptibility, and INS data on each compound.

Hidden assumption

We are using the giant-spin Hamiltonian — strictly valid only when SOC and crystal-field splittings exceed the relevant thermal and microwave scales. For lanthanide ions with $J_\text{eff}$ structure (Tb3+: $^7F_6$; Dy3+: $^6H_{15/2}$) the truncation to the ground multiplet is standard but should be validated against full multiplet calculations.

3 · Vanadyl complexes — VO(TPP), VOPc

V4+ · S=1/2

Electron spin

The vanadyl ion VO2+ hosts a single unpaired electron in a $d_{xy}$ orbital, giving $S=1/2$. With $g_\parallel \approx 1.985$, $g_\perp \approx 1.973$, the Zeeman frequency at 0.34 T is ~9.5 GHz (X-band EPR, conveniently in the microwave window).

51V · I=7/2

Nuclear spin

Vanadium has a single stable isotope 51V with nuclear spin $I=7/2$ (99.75% abundance). This locks in an eight-line hyperfine structure that the chemist cannot remove, but it also provides a built-in quantum register ($\dim=8$ per molecule).

$T_2$

Coherence

$T_2$ ranges from ~1 µs at 80 K (VOPc thin films) to over 0.7 ms at 10 K in nuclear-spin-free CS2 matrices (Zadrozny 2015). Vanadyl phthalocyanine has been demonstrated to host coherence at room temperature.

The simplified Hamiltonian for one vanadyl molecule is:

$$ H_\text{VO} = \mu_B\, B_0 (g_\parallel S_z\cos\theta + g_\perp(S_x\sin\theta\cos\phi + S_y\sin\theta\sin\phi)) + A_\parallel S_z I_z + A_\perp (S_x I_x + S_y I_y) + \mu_N g_{51\text{V}} B_0 I_z . $$

Why vanadyl is the workhorse

Simplest possible electronic structure (S=1/2), microwave-accessible Zeeman frequency, well-known synthesis, benchmark T2 in the µs–ms range. It is the natural first molecule in this roadmap. Lanthanides are the natural second.

4 · Lanthanide SMMs — TbPc2, Dy-SIMs

Lanthanide single-molecule magnets carry total angular momentum $J$ from strong spin–orbit coupling. The crystal field splits the ground $J$ multiplet into doublets/singlets — this internal structure is rich enough to support qudit encodings and clock transitions.

$$ H_\text{Ln} = \mu_B\, \mathbf{B}\cdot \mathbf{g}_J \cdot \mathbf{J} + \sum_{k=2,4,6}\sum_{q=-k}^{k} B_k^q\, O_k^q(\mathbf{J}) + \mathbf{J}\cdot \mathbf{A}_J \cdot \mathbf{I}. $$
TbPc2

Tb3+ · J = 6

Ising-like crystal field, $m_J = \pm 6$ ground doublet, large magnetic moment. Built-in 159Tb nuclear spin $I=3/2$ provides four-line hyperfine clock transitions (Shiddiq 2016).

Dy-SIMs

Dy3+ · J = 15/2

Strongly axial systems (Goodwin 2017) with blocking temperatures above 60 K. $16$-level manifold offers natural multi-level encodings — promising for fault-tolerant qudit codes.

Clock transitions

Decoherence-protected

At specific field values $\partial\omega/\partial B = 0$ — to first order the transition frequency is insensitive to magnetic noise, boosting $T_2$. Use these as the operational points.

Modelling cost

A single Dy3+ ion needs $\dim = 16$ for the electronic ground multiplet alone, and another $\times 8$ for 163Dy ($I=5/2$, 25% abundance) hyperfine if isotope-engineered. With the M3 milestone in place, each lanthanide site becomes a $d=16\text{–}96$ End in the TTN. Bond dimension grows accordingly — cavity-centred topology (M9) is essential here.

5 · Decoherence channels

For LindbladTTN purposes, the spin couples to three dominant baths:

ChannelMechanismLindblad operatorTypical timescale
Spin–lattice ($T_1$) Direct, Raman, Orbach processes via phonons $L_1 = \sqrt{\Gamma_1}\, S_-$ 1 µs at RT → 1 s at sub-K
Pure dephasing ($T_\phi$) Spectral diffusion from nuclear spin bath + spin–phonon $L_\phi = \sqrt{\Gamma_\phi}\, S_z$ µs–ms depending on matrix
Spectral diffusion Slow drift of nuclear spin configuration Modelled via stochastic detuning $\delta(t)$ on $S_z$ Long-time ms drift

LindbladTTN handles arbitrary $(γ_k, L_k)$ pairs as L_terms=[(gamma, {dof: op}), ...] in the heterogeneous API, or as raw L_ops matrices in the legacy spin-1/2 path. M1 + M3 ship today, so these are expressible directly in the spin-$S$ basis with no Pauli decomposition needed.

6 · In code: building the spin site

The vanadyl qubit on the v0.3.0 API — using the vanadyl_spin template that ships with M4:

import numpy as np
from lindblad_ttn import LindbladTTN
from lindblad_ttn.templates import vanadyl_spin

# Electron + 51V nucleus (I = 7/2) site bundle with T1/T2.
sites, H_terms, L_terms = vanadyl_spin(
    omega_q=9.5e9,        # X-band Larmor (Hz)
    A_iso=300e6,          # hyperfine (Hz)
    T1=1e-3, T2=7e-4,     # 1 ms / 700 µs
    include_nucleus=True, # adds 51V I=7/2 site
)

solver = LindbladTTN(
    sites=sites, H_terms=H_terms, L_terms=L_terms,
    bond_dim=16, topology="train", strategy="ps1",
)

Equivalently, building the sites manually with the M1 / M3 API:

from lindblad_ttn import LindbladTTN
from lindblad_ttn.sites import spin_half_site, spin_site

elec = spin_half_site("vo")            # electron S=1/2
nuc  = spin_site(3.5, "I_V51")         # nuclear I=7/2

omega_q = 9.5e9   # Hz
A_iso   = 300e6
T1, T2  = 1e-3, 7e-4

H_terms = [
    (0.5 * omega_q, {elec.name: elec.sz}),
    (A_iso, {elec.name: elec.sx, nuc.name: nuc.Sx}),
    (A_iso, {elec.name: elec.sy, nuc.name: nuc.Sy}),
    (A_iso, {elec.name: elec.sz, nuc.name: nuc.Sz}),
]
gamma_phi = max(0.0, 1.0/(2*T2) - 1.0/(2*T1))
L_terms = [
    (1/T1,     {elec.name: elec.sm}),
    (gamma_phi,{elec.name: elec.sz}),
]

solver = LindbladTTN(
    sites=[elec, nuc],
    H_terms=H_terms, L_terms=L_terms,
    bond_dim=16, topology="train",
)
