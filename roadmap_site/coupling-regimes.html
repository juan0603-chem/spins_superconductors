Coupling regimes

How spins talk to resonators — Jaynes–Cummings, Tavis–Cummings, dispersive, ultrastrong, Kerr-nonlinear, and longitudinal couplings, with the boundaries between them.

1 · Jaynes–Cummings — one spin, one cavity

The canonical building block: a two-level system (spin) coupled to a single bosonic mode (cavity) in the rotating-wave approximation.

$$ H_\text{JC} = \omega_c\, a^\dagger a + \tfrac{1}{2}\omega_q\, \sigma_z + g\,(a^\dagger \sigma^- + a\, \sigma^+). $$

Two regimes are set by the ratio of the coupling $g$ to the detuning $\Delta = \omega_q - \omega_c$:

|Δ| ≪ g

Resonant

Vacuum Rabi oscillations at frequency $2g$. Used for direct iSWAP / SWAP gates and for cavity-mediated quantum-state transfer.

|Δ| ≫ g

Dispersive

Cavity carries a qubit-state-dependent shift $\chi$. The basis of standard cQED readout and the workhorse for CZ-like two-qubit gates.

The RWA is excellent when $g \ll \omega_q, \omega_c$ and $|\Delta| \ll \omega_q + \omega_c$. Outside that window, counter-rotating terms $a\sigma^- + a^\dagger \sigma^+$ matter and you must use the full Rabi Hamiltonian.

2 · Tavis–Cummings — many spins, one cavity

$$ H_\text{TC} = \omega_c\, a^\dagger a + \sum_{i=1}^{N} \tfrac{1}{2}\omega_q^{(i)}\, \sigma_z^{(i)} + \sum_{i=1}^{N} g_i\,(a^\dagger \sigma^-_i + a\, \sigma^+_i). $$

With $N$ identical spins the collective coupling enhances by $\sqrt{N}$: $g_\text{coll} = g\sqrt{N}$. A weak single-spin coupling becomes strong at the ensemble level, opening the door to spin ensemble memories.

For our roadmap, Tavis–Cummings is the natural Hamiltonian for the cavity-centred tree topology (M9): all spins on the leaves, the cavity on the interior, mediated entanglement along the bonds. TTN bond dimension stays low because $H_\text{TC}$ preserves total excitation number — the entanglement is "skinny."

3 · Dispersive regime & the $\chi$ shift

When $|\Delta| \gg g$, eliminate the cavity adiabatically (or use Schrieffer–Wolff to 2nd order — see page 5):

$$ H_\text{disp} \approx \left(\omega_c + \chi\,\sigma_z\right) a^\dagger a + \tfrac{1}{2}\left(\omega_q + \chi\right)\sigma_z, \qquad \chi = \frac{g^2}{\Delta} . $$

The cavity frequency depends on the qubit state — the basis of dispersive readout.

Two spins both dispersively coupled to the same cavity acquire a cavity-mediated exchange:

$$ J_\text{eff} \sim \frac{g_1 g_2}{\Delta} \quad\text{(static)}, \qquad J_\text{eff}(t) \sim \frac{g_1 g_2}{\Delta(t)} \quad\text{(parametric)}. $$
Why this is the operating point

The dispersive regime gives three things at once: state-dependent readout, virtual-photon exchange between spins, and protection from cavity loss (the qubit only borrows the cavity). Almost every two-qubit gate proposed in this roadmap lives here.

Higher-order corrections

Beyond 2nd-order SW, the next term is the qubit Lamb shift $\chi^{(2)} = -g^4 / \Delta^3$ and a photon-number-dependent renormalisation of $\chi$ that limits readout fidelity at high photon number ("critical photon number" $n_\text{crit} = \Delta^2/4g^2$). M5 will deliver these to 4th order programmatically.

4 · Ultrastrong & deep-strong regimes

The coupling strength $g/\omega$ defines four regimes:

NameRangeCounter-rotating terms?RWA?
Weak$g/\omega \lesssim 10^{-3}$negligibleexcellent
Strong$10^{-3} \lesssim g/\omega \lesssim 0.1$smallgood
Ultrastrong$0.1 \lesssim g/\omega \lesssim 1$importantbreaks down
Deep-strong$g/\omega \gtrsim 1$dominantnonsense

In molecular-spin platforms without a SC qubit, $g$ rarely exceeds the kHz range, putting us deep in the weak-coupling regime. With a constriction LC resonator + chemically anchored spin one can reach $g \sim 1\,\text{MHz}$ and start to enter strong coupling.

The transmon-spin coupling is mediated electrically through the same resonator and can reach $g/2\pi \sim 50$–100 MHz. That puts only the transmon in the strong coupling regime; the spin remains weak-to-strong. Ultrastrong is the SC-side ambition for the next decade.

5 · Single-photon Kerr

The Duffing nonlinearity of a transmon (or fluxonium) generates a Kerr term on the cavity it dresses:

$$ H_\text{Kerr} = - \tfrac{K}{2}\, a^\dagger a^\dagger a a, \qquad K \approx \frac{\chi^2}{\alpha} \quad\text{(dispersive limit)} . $$
  • $K$ is the per-photon frequency shift of the cavity.
  • $K \gtrsim \kappa$ enables photon-number-resolved spectroscopy and cat-state encoding in the cavity.
  • $K\, a^\dagger a^\dagger a a$ commutes with $a^\dagger a$ — it conserves photon number, so cat states are stationary.
Why this matters

A cavity with single-photon Kerr is the substrate for bosonic encodings (cat / GKP qubits). In a hybrid platform, encoding the logical qubit in the cavity and using the molecular spin as an ancilla for syndrome extraction is one of the most promising fault-tolerant architectures available. This is well beyond the M1–M10 milestones but worth designing for from the start.

6 · Longitudinal coupling

Instead of the transverse $g(\sigma_+ a + \sigma_- a^\dagger)$, one can engineer a longitudinal coupling:

$$ H_\text{long} = g_z\, \sigma_z\, (a + a^\dagger). $$

Modulating $g_z(t)$ at the cavity frequency turns this into a fast, QND, scalable readout interaction — no virtual photons, no Purcell limit, no critical photon number.

For molecular spins, longitudinal coupling can be engineered by placing the spin in a region of field-gradient rather than uniform field, so that displacement of the spin (via electric driving) modulates $g_z$. This is an experimental frontier — but a clean simulation target once M2 + M3 + M4 land.

The molecular-spin caveat

The intrinsic magnetic-dipole coupling between a single molecular spin and a microwave cavity is genuinely small (~1–10 kHz) for a uniform mode. Reaching strong coupling requires either (a) ensemble enhancement via Tavis–Cummings $\sqrt{N}$, (b) field-concentrating constriction resonators, or (c) coupling through a transmon mediator with electric-dipole coupling. The roadmap supports all three.
